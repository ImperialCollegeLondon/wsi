The pollution concentration in the water cycle, which is influenced by human activity, depends on the combined effects of all the sub-systems that make up the water cycle, as is most evident in rivers. As a result, river catchments are rarely dominated by the behavior of any single sub-system. For instance, 60% of English catchments that do not meet the "good" standard set by the Water Framework Directive fail to do so due to multiple pollution sources, with both urban and rural water cycles contributing to the many problems that rivers face. This implies that a model that focuses on only one sub-system is unlikely to provide accurate estimates of the impacts on other sub-systems. Additionally, estimates within a focused sub-system may be inaccurate if appropriate boundary conditions cannot be defined, which is a problem that water managers are highly concerned about. Therefore, understanding the water cycle as a whole will enable more accurate representations of human sub-systems, their impacts, and the water quality of rivers, which is important for both water supply and biodiversity.

The environmental modeling research community has responded to the challenges of representing the water system by creating integrated computer simulation models. We define an integrated representation as a model that combines sub-system representations that are typically not represented in a coordinated way, in order to capture feedbacks between sub-systems. Approaches to integration range from tightly coupled, where equations and interactions are pre-defined to create a self-contained integrated representation, such as with JULES, to loosely coupled, where sub-system representations are self-contained, and integration occurs by facilitating interactions between sub-systems, acting as a message passing interface, such as with OpenMI. Belete et al. (2017) describes the arrangement of different sub-system components as integrated model orchestration, highlighting that different orchestrations are suitable for different applications. While looser coupling provides greater flexibility in orchestration and therefore a model that can be used to answer a wider variety of questions, it also creates a higher user burden to set up and understand more different sub-systems. In the middle is an integrated representation that offers flexibility in orchestration but comes with self-contained components that do not need to be set up by the user in a burdensome manner, such as CityDrain3. We propose that this middle ground is the most beneficial for the user and believe that the CityDrain3 approach to integration is the most productive way to create user-friendly models of the integrated water cycle.

The main obstacle to using CityDrain3 for whole water cycle simulations is that the software is focused on urban wastewater systems, and no software currently captures the wide range of sub-systems that interact with the water cycle. To understand the water quantity and quality of a given river, we must represent sub-systems such as upstream rivers, reservoirs, freshwater treatment, distribution networks, residential and non-residential water consumers, foul sewers, urban drainage, storm sewers, wastewater treatment works, groundwater, agriculture, and hydrological catchments (and the many physical processes within these catchments). Given this challenge, Zomorodian et al.'s assertion that simply understanding the variety of sub-systems is a key barrier to the adoption of an integrated approach to the water cycle seems unsurprising. Therefore, any representation of the integrated water cycle that is easy to use cannot rely solely on the user's expertise in these numerous sub-systems, but must instead follow the CityDrain3 approach of self-contained representations.

In addition to user expertise, Rauch et al. (2017) identify intense data requirements as another key barrier to the uptake of integrated urban water system modeling, as demonstrated in their impressive DAnCE4Water application. CityDrain3 offers a useful perspective on this problem by providing parsimonious representations of sub-systems, which reduces data requirements and enables users to quickly set up simulations. Calibration may be considered as an alternative to data-driven parameterization, but the concerns of Kirchner et al. (2005) that hydrological catchment models may not give the right results for the right reasons when calibrated will be magnified many times in the case of an integrated representation of the entire water cycle. Iwanaga et al. (2021) instead suggest that characterizing and communicating model uncertainties, and well-documented and ideally open-source code, may help to build trust in simulation results. Whether through formal evaluation of simulations, uncertainty analysis, documentation, or all of the above, an integrated water cycle representation should ideally provide a means to easily set up a model and build trust in its implementation.

This paper introduces the Water Systems Integration MODelling framework (WSIMOD), an open-source Python package that provides physically-based simulations for integrated modeling of the water cycle. WSIMOD addresses the challenges discussed earlier by: 1) integrating subsystem representations through a lightweight and flexible message passing interface, 2) providing a library of built-in components representing different subsystems, and a default orchestration suitable for many catchments, 3) including pre-processing modules with the necessary geospatial operations to parameterize and create model evaluation data for WSIMOD applications. These modules are integrated with English datasets, enabling automatic creation and evaluation of integrated water cycle representations for any English catchment. A demonstration with evaluation against observational data is provided, and extensive model documentation is available online to build trust and familiarity with the WSIMOD process.
