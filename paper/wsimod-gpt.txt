Fluxes in the human-altered water cycle, and their pollution concentration, depends on the sum contribution of all other parts, or subsystems, of the water cycle, which is most evident in rivers. The result is that river catchments are rarely dominated by the behaviour of any specific subsystem. For example, 60% of English catchments that do not achieve a ‘good’ status in the Water Framework Directive do so because of multiple different pollution sources, with both the urban and rural water cycles contributing to the multitude of problems that rivers face. The implication is that a modelled representation focussing on any individual subsystem is unlikely to give accurate estimates of impacts beyond that focussed subsystem. Furthermore, estimates within a focussed subsystem may be inaccurate if sensible boundary conditions cannot be defined, a problem that water managers are highly sensitive to. It follows that understanding the water cycle in a whole picture will allow more accurate representations of human subsystems, their impacts and in particular the water quality of rivers, which we know is important for both water supply and biodiversity.

The response from the environmental modelling research community has been to create integrated representations of the water system in the form of computer simulation models. We define an integrated representation as a representation which joins up subsystem representations that are not typically represented in a joined-up way to capture feedbacks between subsystems. Approaches for integration vary between tightly coupled, where equations and interactions are pre-defined to create a self-contained integrated representation, such as with JULES, and loosely coupled, where subsystem representations are self-contained, and the integration occurs by facilitating interactions between subsystems, filling the role as a message passing interface, such as with OpenMI. Belete et al. (2017) describes the arrangement of different subsystem components as integrated model orchestration, highlighting that different orchestrations will be suitable for different applications. While looser coupling gives a greater degree of flexibility in orchestration, and thus a model that can be used to answer a wider variety of questions, it also creates a higher user burden to set up and understand more different subsystems . In the middle is an integrated representation that gives flexibility around orchestration but comes with self-contained components that do not need to be onerously setup by a user, such as CityDrain3. We propose that this middle ground is the most beneficial for a user and believe that the CityDrain3 approach to integration is the most productive avenue towards creating user-friendly models of the integrated water cycle. 

The key barrier to using CityDrain3 for whole water cycle simulations is that it is beyond the scope of the software, which is focussed on urban wastewater systems. Indeed, no software yet captures the vast array of subsystems that interact with the water cycle. Indeed, to understand the water quantity and quality of a given river, we must represent subsystems including but not limited to upstream rivers, reservoirs, freshwater treatment, distribution networks, residential and non-residential water consumers, foul sewers, urban drainage, storm sewers, wastewater treatment works, groundwater, agriculture, and hydrological catchments (and the many physical processes within these catchments). Faced with this challenge, Zomorodian et al. (2018)’s assertion that simply understanding the variety of subsystems is a key barrier to the uptake of an integrated approach to the water cycle seems unsurprising. Thus, any representation of the integrated water cycle that is relatively easy to use cannot rely only on user expertise in these numerous subsystems and must instead follow the CityDrain3 approach of self-contained representations.

Beyond user expertise, Rauch et al. (2017), in their impressive DAnCE4Water application of integrated urban water system modelling, highlight that another key barrier to uptake is the intense data requirements to parameterise and evaluate such models. CityDrain3 again provides a useful lens to view this problem, opting to provide parsimonious representations of subsystems which eases data requirements and enables a user to quickly set up simulations. Calibration may be considered as one alternative to a data-driven parameterisation, although the concerns of Kirchner et al. (2005) that hydrological catchment models may not give the right results for the right reasons when calibrated, will be magnified many times in the case of an integrated representation of the entire water cycle. Iwanaga et al. (2021) instead suggest that characterising and communicating model uncertainties, and well-documented and ideally open-source code may instead help to reassure model users that they can trust simulation results. Whether through formal evaluation of simulations, uncertainty analysis, documentation, or all, an integrated water cycle representation will ideally provide a means to easily setup a model and build trust in its implementation.

This paper presents the Water Systems Integration MODelling framework (WSIMOD), which is an open-source Python package designed to provide physically based simulations for integrated modelling of the water cycle. It achieves this while tackling the issues raised above in three ways. Firstly, it integrates through a lightweight and flexible integration framework that is a message passing interface to pass information about water quality and quantity between different water cycle subsystem representations. Secondly, it provides an extensive library of built-in components that are different subsystem representations and a default orchestration of these components adjudged to be suitable for many catchments. Where possible these representations are based on parsimonious and peer-reviewed models. Thirdly, pre-processing modules contain the geospatial operations required to parameterise and create model evaluation data for WSIMOD applications. These pre-processing modules are brought together for English datasets, enabling automatic creation and evaluation of integrated water cycle representations for any English catchments. A demonstration with evaluation against observational data is provided. Extensive model documentation is provided online, enabling users to both trust and become familiar with the WSIMOD process.
